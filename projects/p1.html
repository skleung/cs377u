Project 1: That's pretty sketchy

CS 247 takes many of the concepts you learned in introductory design coursework and deepens them. You are no longer an amateur, so we won't be treating you like one. We look forward to seeing you leap over our already-high expectations.

Goal

The goal of P1 is communicating ideas visually.

Design requires communication. The better your communication cuts to the core of the idea, the more effective you are as a designer. It's your responsibility to ensure that others see the same thing in your ideas that you do. If they see something else, you need to accept a mea culpa: there's something you're not communicating clearly. Visual communication is a kind of interface design: if the recipient doesn't rapidly get it, then the interface (communication) needs to be tweaked.

Brief

Your goal is to communicate ideas, interfaces and people fluidly to other designers.

Equip yourself

A good drawing pad or sketchbook is a critical part of the designer's toolkit. Make sure you've purchased a pad and pen you can take with you. Act quickly, because we are not the only class on campus that's asking you to buy a sketchbook and they do sell out.Important features: you must be able to easily tear out the pages (so we can tape them on the walls), no lines, no flimsy pages, no tiny pages (we recommend at least 5"x8"), no thin-lined pens (go for something with a reasonably thick ink stream), and no pencils. The Stanford Bookstore sells drawing pads, as does University Art and Accent Arts. If you get ahead of the game, use Amazon Prime Student to overnight some great ones from there.

Observe and sketch at your location

Your studio instructor will give you a location to go observe and sketch at. Find your assigned studio in the list below.

Once you're at your location, walk around and observe. Take down some sketches to yourself of what you see. Aim to capture at volume, don't worry about filtering to just great observations. Focus on specific stories and details. For example, try telling the story of a single person or set of people at the location. Feel free to take pictures or audio recordings on your phone to complement for your own memory, but do not capture pictures and audio now and then sketch after the fact. You must sketch while you're there.

For this section, you are not graded on your needfinding skills, but instead on your use of visual communication techniques. In other words, are you capturing the important aspects of the environment? Can another designer understand what the situation was, or what you were trying to convey?

The deliverable here is a selection of no fewer than ten pages of your sketches, captured in a primarily visual form. Feel free to include other material as supplements at the end of the assignment submission — we will glance through them too.

If any of the deliverables are missing, we will reduce your score by 25% per deliverable.

Studio Instructor Location
Henriette Cramer (Autonomous things)

Focus on people and their interaction with the things that they encounter in their environment and how they affect each other. Be sure to tell stories with your sketches about the situations that occur.

Option 1: Traffic interactions. Go to a intersection. Watch out please, mind the safety of yourself and others. Sketch how people are interacting with other traffic, as well as their physical (urban) surroundings. Questions to get you started: how do (groups of) people and traffic move, where do they stand/ride, how do they communicate with other traffic. What affects their behavior, are there any hesitations, confusions?

Option 2: Shared goals and individual preferences. Find a restaurant, possibly with an order counter. An information desk/kiosk serving groups in a relatively public space would be fine too. Observe groups of people trying to order/find info together, and groups sitting down. Questions to get you started: Do people need to 'negotiate'? What objects or people's own devices play a role? Do they use or adjust the space around them in some way, for example when they sit down?

Option 3: Your choice. Did the theme spark another idea for a situation you'd like to observe? Feel free...but make sure it's in-depth and in the wild, not an artificial or task-based setting.
Bob Ryskamp (Towards invisible interfaces)
Observe a team sports practice, or a group of people at the gym. Sketch the different types of activities you see, and the interesting interactions. How do people move around the space?
Julie Stanford (Home is where the heart is)

Where to go: To get started with understanding needs in the home, do some observations in a graduate student’s home either on or off campus.The home must be an apartment, condo, or house and cannot be a dorm room. The home you observe must be a private home of some kind with it’s own kitchen and bathroom. Note that row housing like Synergy or La Casa Italiana is still considered dorms even if at some point they were private houses.

How to do it: Ask a graduate student friend (or friend-of-a-friend) if you can come hang out at their house for an hour, observe how they have it set up, and sketch how they go about their business. If they live with other people in the home ask if you can come at a time when more people are around and be a fly on the wall. Families, couples, or roommates are all great opportunities to observe how numerous people engage with their home and with each other.

What to look for: Sketch how things are set up at their house. Take note of what objects are located where and why they might be there. What the relationships between the things in the house and the people? What technologies do you see? What looks old? What looks new? Does anything seem out of place? Intriguing? Mundane? Sketch what and who residents in the home interact with and what they might be feeling. Sketch storyboards of the interactions and vignettes that you see played out. Imagine that later you are going to use your sketches to help illustrate a storybook about what’s going on in this house and why. What would you draw to explain what your seeing to someone else visually?
John Tang (Technology for human-being)

Go to public places where people can exhibit a lot of human-being, such as:

  • Children’s playground (especially at a school during recess)
  • Children’s hospital cafeteria
  • Settings where intergenerational sharing occur (public lounges of senior communities: Avenidas, Littlehouse)
For some places (schools, senior centers), it would be good to check in at the “office” to let them know what you’re doing.

We’re deliberately listing places that involve people in a different demographic than you so you can have fresh eyes to observe the setting. Take special note of anything that’s encouraging or enabling human engagement, or that are creating challenges or obstacles.
Helena Roeber (Leveling the playing field)

Option 1: Diverse Neighborhood. Find a diverse neighborhood or street scene with people. Make sure you are safe. Observe how people of highly differing socio-economic status interact and/or avoid interaction. Observe how the physical layout of the space aids, channels or hinders interactions. Look for objects that aid or hinder interactions. Observe how people’s body language conveys their emotional states and their willingness or unwillingness to interact. Tell mini-stories of your observations with your drawings.

Option 2: Food Pantry. Visit a local food bank that is serving people in need. Please make sure to introduce yourselves to the organizers first and ask if it is ok to stay and observe. Observe the interactions between the clients and the staff, between the clients and between the staff. Observe how the physical space aids, channels or hinders interactions. Look how objects like the food being served influence the interactions. Pay particular attention to the emotional states of the clients and staff. Tell mini-stories of your observations.

Option 3: Your choice. Pick a real world situation of your choice. Make sure it is a location with a diverse set of clientele. Make sure you are safe. If it is not a public place, introduce yourselves and ask if it is ok to stay and sketch. Look for interactions/logistics/objects that bring a diverse set of people together or separate them emotionally. Tell small stories with your drawings.

Jenn Marlow (Destinations)

Observe people visiting a place of interest, either in an organized group or on their own. On campus, this could include people taking a Stanford campus tour, visiting the Cantor Arts Center or Memorial Church. Or feel free to venture further afield to popular spots in Palo Alto or beyond that you would consider a tourist "destination”. If you’re taking part in a tour, it might be a good idea to introduce yourself to the guide and explain why you’re there.

Things to look for: How are people using technology in the place and over time? How are they interacting with each other? What aspects of the tour/place seem to be most interesting to different people? What aspects of the experience do they seem to be trying to capture?

Grading rubric

An important thing to keep in mind for this advanced class is that full credit is not automatic, nor very common. (There will of course be a curve at the end.) Nobody is perfect at design at first.

Remember that there are no unit tests for design. In the real world, evaluation is a subjective but highly expert practice. We ensure fair grading by having both the TA and the studio instructor, who has years of experience, review your submission.
