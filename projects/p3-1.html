Project 3: Design Sprint

Part I

Form a team

Form a team of three from your studio. We suggest that you seek out teammates who bring together complementary skills and who have compatible schedules and priorities. If your studio cannot divide evenly into groups of three, talk to the studio staff about options. You are welcome to choose your own team, but for the best experience we recommend working with a different team than P2. You will stick with this team for the rest of the quarter, executing the final project across both P3 and P4.

Once you've formed a team, make it official by registering it on this Google Sheet ASAP. Find your instructors' initials and write the full names of your team members in a blank row.

Write a needfinding plan

You are welcome to choose any direction that fits within the studio theme. You may take ideas from lecture, or brainstorm your own. Remember that it may take some time to set up the interviews and the trip, so start early. We may be able to help connect you with relevant user groups. Look past the people and environments that are readily accessible: focus on opportunities that will teach us something. For example, if you're studying health, Vaden is nearby but doesn't give you new perspectives, so avoid it. To help your selection, identify relevant stakeholders â€” people directly involved or implicated in the activity. For example, if you are interested in elder care, potential stakeholders include the elderly being cared for, family members, and health care professionals.

Decide on a needfinding plan, focusing on with whom and where you want to do your needfinding. Produce a shot list of your stakeholders, including how or where you might find them, interview them, and observe them. What do you hope to learn? Who will you talk to? What will you be sketching? This should be at least two solid paragraphs. The d.school bootcamp bootleg has good ideas about different ways to frame your needfinding. Example helpful sections include interview preparation and extreme users.

Next, put together a discussion guide of questions and topics to cover when you go out on your interviews. The discussion guide should record verbatim your introduction, the questions you are going to ask, what activities (if any) you are planning on doing with the participants, and (if any) survey questions you will be distributing over the web. The verbatim part is key because if you ask leading questions, the needfinding will be useless. The Asking Questions section in Patnaik should help.

You should have at least one interview per person on your team, but we ask you to do more. The more interviews you do, the more interesting insights you will find.

Submit

Pull everything together into your submission for P3.I on Canvas. This includes:

  • A text file with your team members' names and SUNet IDs.
  • A document detailing your topic of interest, your needfinding plan, and your discussion guide.

Have one of your group memebers submit these documents on Canvas.

Be prepared to present your needfinding plan and discussion guide in studio in three to four minutes. Bring a printout of your needfinding plan and discussion guide with you to studio.

Grading rubric

Each milestone is worth five points. You will receive five points for completing the assignment satisfactorily, submitting it, and your whole team bringing your deliverables to class for feedback.
